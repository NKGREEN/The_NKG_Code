Thank you for taking a peak at the inaugural issue of The {NKG} Code. This newsletter will chronicle the trials, tribulations, and epic lightsaber battles of a former ESL teacher beginning her second act as a front end web developer.

FEATURE: Professor, Teach Thyself

I am a web developer. Previously, I worked as an English as a Second Language adjunct instructor for over ten years. It started out great with a one-year stint in Tokyo, Japan, teaching at a language school. It turned from a fun way to travel abroad into a career when I returned stateside with surprisingly few job opportunities being presented to me as an English Literature major.

I decided to debug my life (hold on tight, more cheesy jokes to follow!) and learn web development. I needed to get excited about learning again, and web development seemed like the creative outlet I needed.

Coding requires learning languages. I taught language, and speak anime-centric Japanese, So I should take to coding like the proverbial duck to water, right? RIGHT?!?

Functions and Loops and Arrays, Oh My!

Well, not quite.

I think my experience has made me open to the idea of not knowing things, and being comfortable with that as an adult. But it has not alleviated the anxiety I feel when I don't get something on the first go. Or second, or third (I'm talking about you, JavaScript Currying!)

Learning is, of course, it's own reward, but sometimes it is hard to appreciate that when you want to be rewarded with some divs being creatively aligned on a page like comic book panels. Or a cookie. Which you feel guilty about having if they aren't served on a plate of functioning, beautiful code.

I've Got 99 Problems and Imposter Syndrome is ALL OF THEM

Well, 97 of them. I also have tennis elbow from a quite physically-demanding retail job that is just barely paying the bills until I get a developer gig, and a vitamin B12 deficiency after switching to a plant-based lifestyle. But those 97...they keep me up at night. Perhaps it is the old "girls are to math/technology as oil is to water" myth, or just not having the opportunity to sit down and learn something new from scratch in quite some time, but I feel both terrified and guilty for committing myself to this pursuit. While I love every snippet of code I write (the act of coding, not necessarily the output...yet), I sometimes think it is an exercise in failure, that I will never be able to create a beautiful CodePen animation purely out of CSS, or work on a team to build an exciting landing page for an innovative start-up. And if this is the case...wouldn't I be better served to devote my study time to my family? Pick up another teaching gig because, hey, I already have a Masters in that field. Plenty of people earn money from unfulfilling careers. Why should I be any different?

Knowing is Half the Battle

Because. I am different.

It's always daunting to switch gears and learn something new (elderly canine comparisons do NOT help). But it's important to have a clear understanding of what you would like to accomplish, the skills that are required for that position/goal, and a solid awareness of your gaps in understanding.

  • Learn the art of "Google-Fu"! You can't expect the answers to just be given to you, but know that they are there at your fingertips.Keep trudging forward in your search for the perfect way to render inline elements, no matter how many rude comments your question gets on Stack Overflow.
  • Find a group of like-minded individuals, who can relate to your challenges. I've found that in my Moms Can & Co cohort. ü•∞

    • Knowing that my fellow cohort members truly understand my struggles, and can serve up some solution sandwiches is awesome. It lets me know that I am in control of my learning process.

    Code or Code Not. There is No Try.

    2020: new year, new vision for my life. I'm going to stay the course, remembering that there is a better life on the horizon; a fulfilling career, greater work-life balance, and more time to explore the possibilities of... possibilities!
